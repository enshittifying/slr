Until June 28, 2013, many court decisions published in the Netherlands received a National Reports Number (LJN), consisting of two letters and four digits. After that date, decisions are to have a European Case Law Identifier (ECLI), a standard that was adopted in late 2010. For purposes of conversion into ECLI coding, the LJN is included in the final (numeric) part of ECLI, e.g., ECLI:NL:GHAMS:2014:566, where NL is for Netherlands and GHAMS is Gerechtshof Amsterdam. A search for cases on websites such as Rechtspraak.nl, https://uitspraken.rechtspraak.nl/#!/, can be done using the identifier.

Ordinary jurisdiction

Hoge Raad der Nederlanden (HR) (Supreme Court of the Netherlands), Gerechtshof (Hof) (ordinary courts of appeals), Hoog Militair Gerechtshof (HMG) (Military Court of Appeals) (abolished in 1991), and Krijgsraad (Kr.) (Court Martial) (no longer in existence): Cite to NJ or W, if therein.

Prior to 2002, there were two courts of first instance: Arrondissementsrechtbank (Rb.) (ordinary court of first instance and court of appeals to the Kantongerecht) and Kantongerecht (Ktg.) (court of first instance for labor, rent, and misdemeanor cases). In 2002, these two courts merged, eliminating the latter court as a separate court (instead, it is now a subdivision of the former court). Currently, labor, rent, and small claims (rather than misdemeanor) cases are still decided by a different kind of judge—the Kantonrechter (Ktr.)—than are other civil cases. Because of the merger, the Arrondissementsrechtbank (Rb.) is no longer a court of appeals to the Kantongerecht (Ktg.). After the merger, those specific types of cases should be cited to the Kantonrechter (Ktr.) instead of the Kantongerecht (Ktg.), following the form mentioned above. Cite to Prg. or NJF, if therein.

The Staatsblad van het Koninkrijk der Nederlanden (Stb., sometimes cited as S.) is the official gazette of the Kingdom of the Netherlands. It is the official source for finding and citing the authoritative text of all codes, laws, general administrative measures, and royal decrees.

The Nederlandse Staatscourant (Stcrt.) is an official daily journal used for the publication of some royal decrees, ministerial decrees, ministerial circulars, and other official statements.

A parallel citation to Nedersandse Staatswetten (Schuurman & Jordens) or Nederlandse Wetgeving (Cremers) may be given.

Internet sources

Legislation passed since January 1, 1995 that has been published in the Staatsblad or the Staatscourant is available on the government’s website at https://www.officielebekendmakingen.nl. Treaties and Parliamentary Papers are also available from this site. This site gives further access to all Ministries and other government organizations.

The de Rechtspraak website, available at http://www.rechtspraak.nl, provides free access to cases.

Decisions of the Administrative Court of Highest Instance are searchable and available in full text at http://www.raadvanstate.nl/uitspraken.html.

Parliamentary documents from 1814–1995 for both houses of the Dutch Parliament (Staten-Generaal) are available on its website, at Staten-Generaal Digitaal, http://www.statengeneraaldigitaal.nl/.

The website of the Eerste Kamer (First Chamber of the Parliament) is http://www.eerstekamer.nl/home.

The website of the Tweede Kamer (Second Chamber of the Parliament) is http://www.tweedekamer.nl/.

Comprehensive online access to legislation may be obtained through a paid subscription to the SDU online database, http://www.sdu.nl, or to the Lexplicatie database offered by Kluwer, http://www.lexplicatie.nl/.

The general government website, http://www.government.nl/, provides links to the individual ministries and includes more than two hundred documents and publications, among other features.

The website of the Ministry of Security and Justice is https://www .rijksoverheid.nl/ministeries/ministerie-van-justitie-en-veiligheid.

The online portal Boom Legal (Boom Juridische, or BJu) Publishers has articles from twenty-eight Dutch law journals, available by paid subscription to one or more, and five open access journals: BJu Tijdschriften, http://www.bjutijdschriften.nl/pagina/informatie.

The commercial website Dutch Civil Law (DCL) provides “free information online in English on the principles and provisions of Dutch Civil Law, the Dutch legal order and the rules of private international law of the Netherlands,” http://www.dutchcivillaw.com/. The website’s translations are done by DCL unless otherwise noted and are not official.

The Advisory Administrative Law Foundation (Stichting Adviesgroep Bestuursrecht, St-AB) has a website devoted to laws on social security, with a view to providing free legal assistance, http://www.st-ab.nl/index.html.
