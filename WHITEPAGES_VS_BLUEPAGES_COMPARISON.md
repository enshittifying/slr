# Whitepages vs Bluepages: Complete Typeface Rule Comparison

## FUNDAMENTAL DISTINCTION

### What Are Whitepages and Bluepages?

**WHITEPAGES (Pages 1-160 of Bluebook)**
- **Context:** Academic legal writing
- **Usage:** Law review articles, journal articles, scholarly papers
- **Audience:** Academics, students, legal scholars
- **Citation Style:** Full, complex citations with extensive typeface variation
- **Location in Bluebook:** The introductory pages (printed on white paper in physical editions)

**BLUEPAGES (Pages 161+ of Bluebook)**
- **Context:** Practitioner/Court document writing
- **Usage:** Court briefs, motions, memoranda, legal documents filed with courts
- **Audience:** Judges, opposing counsel, court staff
- **Citation Style:** Simplified, minimal typeface variation
- **Location in Bluebook:** The practitioner section (printed on blue paper in physical editions)

## RULE 2 COMPARISON TABLE

| Aspect | Whitepages (BB 2.0-2.2) | Bluepages (RB 2.0-2.3) |
|--------|--------------------------|------------------------|
| **Primary Purpose** | Convey meaning through typeface variation | Professional, clean appearance |
| **Typefaces Used** | 4 (ordinary roman, italics, SMALL CAPS, LARGE & SMALL CAPS) | Primarily roman, minimal variation |
| **Citation Footnotes** | Extensive typeface rules | Simplified rules |
| **Emphasis Method** | Italics (used moderately) | Avoid formatting; use substantive writing |
| **Bold Usage** | Not for emphasis | Extremely rare/extraordinary only |
| **Underlining** | Not typical | Minimal, specific elements only |
| **Small Caps** | Extensive (periodicals, institutions, codes) | Essentially non-existent |
| **Main Text Format** | Roman + Italics only | Roman + minimal variation |
| **Professional Tone** | Academic, formatted | Conservative, clean |

## DETAILED RULE-BY-RULE COMPARISON

### RULE 2.1 COMPARISON

#### WHITEPAGES: Typeface Conventions for Citations (BB 2.1)

**Applies to:** Law review footnotes and citations

**ITALICS:**
- Case names (including procedural phrases)
- Book titles
- Titles of periodicals (including student-written works)

**ORDINARY ROMAN:**
- Court document titles (when referred to in text)
- Legislative material titles
- Explanatory phrases
- Introductory signals
- Prior or subsequent history phrases

**SMALL CAPS:**
- Institutional author names
- Titles of regulations in a code

**LARGE & SMALL CAPS:**
- Used in specific contexts (less common)

---

#### BLUEPAGES: Underlined Type (RB 2.1)

**Applies to:** Court filings, briefs, practitioner documents

**UNDERLINED or ITALICIZED (Minimal Use):**
- Some textual case names
- Book titles
- In certain specific documents (see RB 16.5, 18.7)

**RESTRICTIONS:**
- Do NOT underline text for emphasis
- Do NOT underline punctuation adjacent to underlined text

**NO SMALL CAPS:**
- Small caps are essentially never used in practitioner documents

---

### RULE 2.2 COMPARISON

#### WHITEPAGES: Typeface Conventions for Textual Material (BB 2.2)

**Applies to:** Main text of law review pieces (not citations)

**TYPEFACES ALLOWED:**
- Ordinary roman type
- Italics only

**ITALICIZED:**
- Case names (including "v." and procedural phrases)
- Titles of publications, speeches, articles
- Words for emphasis or style (sparingly)

**ORDINARY ROMAN:**
- All other material (vast majority of text)

**CITATION CONTEXT:**
- In footnote text, citations are allowed and embedded
- Case names in textual footnote sentences are italicized

---

#### BLUEPAGES: Bolded Type (RB 2.2)

**Applies to:** Court documents and practitioner writing

**BOLD USAGE:**
- Only in Extraordinary Circumstances
- Stanford Law Review may use bold when author's argument explicitly centers on emphasized word
- Sometimes used for Article titles in citations (follow author's lead)

**RESTRICTIONS:**
- Do NOT add bold for emphasis in standard circumstances
- Remove bold if authors add it inappropriately
- Not for general formatting or emphasis

---

### RULE 2.3 COMPARISON

#### WHITEPAGES: (Part of BB 2.1(d))
**Punctuation with Italics**

**RULE:** Italicize punctuation marks only if immediately adjacent to italicized words.

**EXAMPLES:**
- Correct: *Roe v. Wade*, 410 U.S. 113 (period NOT italicized)
- Correct: *See*, Smith v. Jones (comma NOT italicized after See)
- Correct: *In re* Smith (preposition/word attached)

---

#### BLUEPAGES: Italics and Punctuation (RB 2.3)

**RULE:** Same as Whitepages - Italicize punctuation marks only if immediately adjacent.

**PRACTICE NOTE:** In practitioner documents, italicizing is rare, so this rule has minimal practical application.

## ELEMENT-BY-ELEMENT COMPARISON

### CASE NAMES

| Context | Whitepages | Bluepages |
|---------|------------|-----------|
| In text | *Roe v. Wade* (italics) | Roe v. Wade or _Roe v. Wade_ (minimal) |
| In citation | *Roe v. Wade*, 410 U.S. 113 | *Roe v. Wade*, 410 U.S. 113 |
| With procedural phrase | *In re* Smith | In re Smith (minimal styling) |
| In parenthetical | (quoting *Smith v. Jones*, 123 U.S. 456) | (quoting Smith v. Jones, 123 U.S. 456) |
| Emphasis | Always italics | Minimal, may not italicize |

**Pattern:** Whitepages: Always italics. Bluepages: Minimal, context-dependent.

---

### BOOK TITLES

| Context | Whitepages | Bluepages |
|---------|------------|-----------|
| In citation | *DEMOCRACY AND DISTRUST* | Democracy and Distrust (or _underlined_) |
| In text | *Democracy and Distrust* | Democracy and Distrust (or underlined) |
| With author | JOHN HART ELY, *DEMOCRACY AND DISTRUST* | John Hart Ely, Democracy and Distrust |
| Emphasis | Italics | Minimal/underline |

**Pattern:** Whitepages: Italics. Bluepages: Roman or minimal underlining.

---

### PERIODICAL NAMES

| Context | Whitepages | Bluepages |
|---------|------------|-----------|
| In citation | 110 STAN. L. REV. 1 (2020) | 110 Stan. L. Rev. 1 (2020) |
| Full form | 110 STANFORD LAW REVIEW 1 | 110 Stanford Law Review 1 |
| Emphasis | SMALL CAPS (always) | Never SMALL CAPS |
| Example 1 | 91 HARV. L. REV. 501 | 91 Harv. L. Rev. 501 |
| Example 2 | 100 YALE L.J. 1 | 100 Yale L.J. 1 |

**Pattern:** Whitepages: SMALL CAPS mandatory. Bluepages: Roman type, no SMALL CAPS.

---

### INSTITUTIONAL AUTHOR NAMES

| Context | Whitepages | Bluepages |
|---------|------------|-----------|
| In citation | AMERICAN BAR ASSOCIATION | American Bar Association |
| Full name | AMERICAN BAR ASSOCIATION | American Bar Association |
| Abbreviation | A.B.A. or AMERICAN BAR ASS'N | A.B.A. or American Bar Association |
| Emphasis | SMALL CAPS | Roman type |
| Example | RESTATEMENT (THIRD) OF TORTS | Restatement (Third) of Torts |

**Pattern:** Whitepages: SMALL CAPS. Bluepages: Roman type.

---

### SIGNALS

| Context | Whitepages | Bluepages |
|---------|------------|-----------|
| In citations | See Smith v. Jones... | See Smith v. Jones... |
| Signals format | See, Cf., But see (roman) | See, Cf., But see (roman) |
| As verb in text | *See* the issue as complex | See the issue as complex (roman) |
| Emphasis | No emphasis on signals | No emphasis on signals |
| Example 1 | See Brown v. Board | See Brown v. Board |
| Example 2 | Cf. Doe v. Bolton | Cf. Doe v. Bolton |

**Pattern:** Both use roman type in citations. Whitepages: Italics when signal is a verb in text. Bluepages: Minimal use of verb signals.

---

### COURT DOCUMENTS REFERENCED

| Context | Whitepages | Bluepages |
|---------|------------|-----------|
| In text | Plaintiff's Motion for Summary Judgment | Plaintiff's Motion for Summary Judgment |
| Emphasis | Roman type | Roman type |
| In citation | See Plaintiff's Motion for Summary Judgment | See Plaintiff's Motion for Summary Judgment |
| Title format | Roman type (not italics) | Roman type |

**Pattern:** Both use roman type. Whitepages: Consistent rule. Bluepages: Always roman.

---

### EMPHASIS/STYLISTIC USAGE

| Aspect | Whitepages | Bluepages |
|---------|------------|-----------|
| Italics for emphasis | Allowed sparingly | Not recommended |
| Bold for emphasis | Not used | Extremely rare (extraordinary) |
| Underlining for emphasis | Not used | Not used |
| General approach | Use sparingly | Avoid formatting tricks |
| Professional preference | Substance over form | Substance over form |
| Example: Emphasis | This is *crucial* (sparing) | This is crucial. (no emphasis) |
| Example: Important words | The term *malice* | The term malice |

**Pattern:** Whitepages: Italics sparingly allowed. Bluepages: Avoid all formatting for emphasis.

---

### PUNCTUATION TREATMENT

| Aspect | Whitepages | Bluepages |
|---------|------------|-----------|
| Rule | Italicize if immediately adjacent | Same rule (rarely applied) |
| Application | Common in citations | Minimal (few italics used) |
| Example 1 | *Roe v. Wade*. (period not italicized) | Roe v. Wade. (period not italicized) |
| Example 2 | *See*, Smith v. Jones | See, Smith v. Jones |
| Example 3 | *In re* Smith (word attached) | In re Smith |
| Comma treatment | Roman if space present | Roman if space present |

**Pattern:** Both follow same rule; Whitepages applies it more frequently due to more italicizing.

## COMPARATIVE EXAMPLES

### Example 1: Case Citation

**Whitepages (Law Review):**
```
1. Roe v. Wade, 410 U.S. 113, 115 (1973).
```
- Case name: *Italicized*
- Reporter and page: Roman
- Parenthetical: Roman

**Bluepages (Court Document):**
```
See Roe v. Wade, 410 U.S. 113, 115 (1973).
```
- Case name: Roman or underlined (minimal styling)
- Reporter and page: Roman
- Overall: Clean, professional

---

### Example 2: Book Citation

**Whitepages (Law Review):**
```
2. JOHN HART ELY, DEMOCRACY AND DISTRUST (1980).
```
- Author name: Roman
- Book title: *Italicized*
- Publication info: Roman

**Bluepages (Court Document):**
```
See John Hart Ely, Democracy and Distrust (1980).
```
- Author name: Roman
- Book title: Roman or underlined
- Publication info: Roman
- Overall: Simplified

---

### Example 3: Periodical Citation

**Whitepages (Law Review):**
```
3. John Smith, The Future of Privacy, 110 STAN. L. REV. 1 (2020).
```
- Author: Roman
- Article title: *Italicized*
- Periodical name: SMALL CAPS
- Volume/page: Roman

**Bluepages (Court Document):**
```
See John Smith, The Future of Privacy, 110 Stan. L. Rev. 1 (2020).
```
- Author: Roman
- Article title: Roman
- Periodical name: Roman (no SMALL CAPS)
- Volume/page: Roman
- Overall: All roman type

---

### Example 4: Text with Case Citation

**Whitepages (Law Review - Main Text):**
```
In Roe v. Wade, 410 U.S. 113 (1973), the Court established the right to privacy.
This landmark decision in *Roe* fundamentally changed constitutional law.
```
- Case name in text: *Italicized*
- Case name in citation footnote: *Italicized*
- Short form: *Roe* (italicized)

**Bluepages (Court Document):**
```
In Roe v. Wade, 410 U.S. 113 (1973), the Court established the right to privacy.
This landmark decision in Roe fundamentally changed constitutional law.
```
- Case name: Roman or minimal styling
- Overall: Professional, clean

---

### Example 5: Emphasis in Text

**Whitepages (Law Review):**
```
This issue is particularly *important* to the analysis. The term *mens rea*
requires careful interpretation.
```
- Emphasized word: *Italics*
- Foreign word: *Italics*
- Style: Sparse, selective

**Bluepages (Court Document):**
```
This issue is particularly important to the analysis. The term mens rea
requires careful interpretation.
```
- No emphasis formatting
- No italics
- Style: Professional, undecorated

## KEY DIFFERENCES SUMMARY

### 1. Complexity
- **Whitepages:** 4 typefaces (roman, italics, SMALL CAPS, LARGE & SMALL CAPS)
- **Bluepages:** Primarily roman, minimal variation

### 2. Case Names
- **Whitepages:** Always italicized
- **Bluepages:** Minimal styling (roman or underline)

### 3. Periodical Names
- **Whitepages:** SMALL CAPS (mandatory)
- **Bluepages:** Roman type (no SMALL CAPS)

### 4. Institutional Names
- **Whitepages:** SMALL CAPS
- **Bluepages:** Roman type

### 5. Emphasis
- **Whitepages:** Italics allowed (sparingly)
- **Bluepages:** Avoid emphasis formatting

### 6. Bold
- **Whitepages:** Not used
- **Bluepages:** Extremely rare (extraordinary circumstances only)

### 7. Underlining
- **Whitepages:** Not typical
- **Bluepages:** Minimal use (for case names, titles in some contexts)

### 8. Professional Tone
- **Whitepages:** Academic, formatted, meaning-through-typeface
- **Bluepages:** Conservative, clean, substance-focused

### 9. Target Audience
- **Whitepages:** Academics, students, scholars
- **Bluepages:** Judges, courts, opposing counsel

### 10. Rule Application Philosophy
- **Whitepages:** Use formatting to convey meaning and adhere to tradition
- **Bluepages:** Minimize formatting; focus on substance and professional presentation

## WHEN TO USE WHITEPAGES vs BLUEPAGES

### Use WHITEPAGES (BB 2.0-2.2) When:
- Writing for law review or academic journal
- Composing scholarly article or student comment
- Citations appear in footnotes
- Your audience is primarily academics
- Your document is primarily textual with supporting citations
- You need to convey meaning through typeface variation
- You want to adhere to traditional academic citation styles

### Use BLUEPAGES (RB 2.0-2.3) When:
- Writing briefs, motions, or memoranda
- Submitting documents to a court
- Preparing legal documents for professional practice
- Your audience is judges and court staff
- You need a clean, professional appearance
- Minimal formatting is preferred
- Citations are integrated into professional legal writing

## DECISION MATRIX

```
Question: What document type am I writing?

├─ Law Review Article / Academic Paper
│  └─ USE WHITEPAGES (BB 2.0-2.2)
│     ├─ Case names: *Italicize*
│     ├─ Periodicals: SMALL CAPS
│     ├─ Institutions: SMALL CAPS
│     ├─ Emphasis: Italics (sparingly)
│     └─ Overall complexity: HIGH
│
├─ Court Brief / Motion / Legal Memorandum
│  └─ USE BLUEPAGES (RB 2.0-2.3)
│     ├─ Case names: Roman or minimal styling
│     ├─ Periodicals: Roman
│     ├─ Institutions: Roman
│     ├─ Emphasis: Avoid formatting
│     └─ Overall complexity: LOW
│
└─ Uncertain?
   ├─ Is your document going to be filed with a court?
   │  └─ YES → BLUEPAGES
   │  └─ NO → Probably WHITEPAGES
   │
   └─ Is your audience primarily judges/lawyers?
      └─ YES → BLUEPAGES (unless law review)
      └─ NO → WHITEPAGES
```

## COMMON PITFALLS

### Mistake 1: Using SMALL CAPS in Court Documents
```
Wrong: See the AMERICAN BAR ASSOCIATION guidelines...
Right: See the American Bar Association guidelines...
```

### Mistake 2: Using Whitepages Style in Court Brief
```
Wrong: *Roe v. Wade*, 410 U.S. 113 (using full italicization)
Right: Roe v. Wade, 410 U.S. 113 (cleaner for court)
```

### Mistake 3: Minimal Formatting in Law Review
```
Wrong: Roe v. Wade decided the case. (no italicization)
Right: *Roe v. Wade* decided the case. (italicized in academic context)
```

### Mistake 4: Bold for Emphasis in Either Context
```
Wrong: This is **very important** in law review
Wrong: This is **very important** in court brief
Right: This is important. (Let substance speak)
```

### Mistake 5: Confusing Periodical Formatting
```
Wrong (Whitepages): 110 Stanford Law Review 1 (no SMALL CAPS)
Right (Whitepages): 110 STAN. L. REV. 1 (SMALL CAPS)

Wrong (Bluepages): 110 STAN. L. REV. 1 (SMALL CAPS in court doc)
Right (Bluepages): 110 Stan. L. Rev. 1 (Roman)
```

## REFERENCE QUICK LOOKUP

### If Your Document Is:

**Law Review Article**
- Case names: *Italics*
- Periodicals: SMALL CAPS
- Institutions: SMALL CAPS
- Signals: Roman (italics if verb in text)
- Emphasis: Sparingly with italics

**Court Brief**
- Case names: Roman
- Periodicals: Roman
- Institutions: Roman
- Signals: Roman
- Emphasis: Avoid formatting

**Memorandum (Internal)**
- Follow document convention; usually like court brief (Bluepages style)

**Scholarly Article**
- Follow Whitepages rules
- Case names: *Italics*
- Periodicals: SMALL CAPS
- Full typeface variation allowed
